Neck pain is one of the most common musculoskeletal complaints in the world, affecting up to 70% of people at some point in their lives. Whether it's a dull ache after a long day at the desk, sharp pain when turning your head, or persistent stiffness that just won't go away, neck pain can significantly impact your quality of life.

Understanding Your Cervical Spine

Neck pain refers to any pain or discomfort in the cervical spine region — the seven vertebrae (C1–C7) that support your head and enable movement. Neck pain can range from a minor nuisance to a severely limiting condition, depending on its cause and severity. It is broadly classified into two types:

  1. Acute neck pain — lasting under 12 weeks, often from injury or poor posture
  2. Chronic neck pain — lasting more than 12 weeks, often related to degenerative changes or ongoing strain

Common Causes of Neck Pain

Poor Posture / Tech Neck

Prolonged forward head posture while using phones and computers places excessive load on the cervical spine.

Muscle Strain

Overuse, sudden movement, or sleeping in an awkward position causes muscle tension and spasm.

Cervical Disc Herniation

A disc bulges and presses on nearby nerve roots, causing pain, numbness, or tingling into the arm.

Cervical Spondylosis

Age-related degeneration of the cervical discs and joints, common in adults over 40.

Whiplash Injury

Rapid back-and-forth neck movement from car accidents or sports trauma, causing ligament and muscle damage.

Cervicogenic Headache

Headaches originating from the upper cervical spine, often felt behind one eye or at the base of the skull.

Signs & Symptoms of Neck Pain

Neck pain presents differently for each person. Common symptoms our physiotherapists assess include:

  • Dull aching or sharp pain in the neck, base of skull, or upper shoulders
  • Stiffness and reduced range of motion, difficulty turning or tilting the head
  • Headaches originating from the neck (cervicogenic headaches)
  • Radiating pain, numbness, or tingling down the arm, hand, or fingers
  • Muscle weakness in the arm or hand
  • Pain that worsens with prolonged sitting, computer use, or driving
  • Grinding or clicking sounds when moving the neck (crepitus)
  • Dizziness or balance issues in some cervical conditions

Seek urgent care if: You experience neck pain following trauma, or pain accompanied by weakness in both arms or legs, difficulty walking, or loss of bladder/bowel control.

4 Physiotherapist-Recommended Exercises for Neck Pain

Important: These exercises are general in nature. Always consult your physiotherapist first, especially if your neck pain is severe or involves arm symptoms.

Exercise 1 — Chin Tucks

  1. Sit or stand tall with shoulders relaxed.
  2. Gently draw your chin straight back (creating a 'double chin').
  3. Hold for 5 seconds. Repeat 10 times, 3 sets daily.

🎯 Targets: deep cervical flexors, posture correction

Exercise 2 — Cervical Rotation Stretch

  1. Sit upright and slowly rotate your head to look over one shoulder.
  2. Hold at end range for 20–30 seconds without forcing.
  3. Return to center, repeat other side.

🎯 Targets: cervical joint mobility & upper trapezius

Exercise 3 — Upper Trapezius Stretch

  1. Sit tall. Place one hand behind your back.
  2. Tilt your head toward the opposite shoulder until a stretch is felt.
  3. Hold 30 seconds. Repeat 3× each side.

🎯 Targets: upper trapezius & levator scapulae

Exercise 4 — Shoulder Blade Squeeze

  1. Sit or stand with arms by your side.
  2. Squeeze your shoulder blades together and slightly downward.
  3. Hold 5 seconds. Repeat 10–15 times.

🎯 Targets: middle & lower trapezius, upper back posture

Frequently Asked Questions About Neck Pain

Most acute neck pain resolves within 4–8 weeks of physiotherapy. Chronic neck pain conditions may take 3–6 months of consistent treatment and exercise. Early intervention produces the fastest recovery.

Self-manipulation of the neck is not recommended. While it may provide temporary relief, repeated self-cracking can cause joint laxity and muscle imbalance over time. A trained physiotherapist can safely mobilise the cervical spine with targeted, controlled techniques.
