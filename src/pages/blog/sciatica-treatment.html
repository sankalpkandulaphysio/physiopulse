What Is Sciatica?

Sciatica is not a diagnosis in itself — it's a symptom caused by irritation or compression of the sciatic nerve, the longest nerve in the human body. The sciatic nerve begins in the lower spine (lumbar region), runs through the buttock, and travels down each leg to the foot.

When this nerve becomes pinched or inflamed, it can cause a range of painful and disruptive symptoms:

  • Sharp, shooting pain from the lower back through the buttock and leg
  • Burning or electric-shock-like sensations along the nerve pathway
  • Numbness or tingling in the leg, foot, or toes
  • Muscle weakness in the affected leg
  • Worsening pain when sitting, standing for long periods, or sneezing

Common Causes of Sciatica

Understanding the root cause of your sciatica is critical to selecting the right treatment. The most common causes include:

Herniated / Bulging Disc

When a spinal disc slips out of place and presses on the sciatic nerve — the most common cause of sciatica.

Spinal Stenosis

Narrowing of the spinal canal due to ageing or arthritis, compressing the nerve roots.

Piriformis Syndrome

The piriformis muscle in the buttock spasms and irritates the nearby sciatic nerve.

Spondylolisthesis

A vertebra slips forward over another, pinching nerve roots and causing radiating pain.

Trauma / Injury

Falls, car accidents, or sports injuries can directly damage or compress the sciatic nerve.

Poor Posture & Prolonged Sitting

Extended sitting loads the lumbar discs unevenly, increasing the risk of disc herniation over time.

How Physiotherapy Treats Sciatica

Physiotherapy is a first-line, evidence-based treatment for sciatica that addresses not just the pain but the underlying cause. Unlike medications that mask symptoms, physiotherapy creates lasting recovery.

At PhysioPulse, our experienced physiotherapists in Mehdipatnam conduct a thorough assessment before creating your personalised treatment plan. Treatment typically includes a combination of the following:

1

Manual Therapy

Hands-on techniques to mobilise and manipulate the spine, sacroiliac joints, and surrounding soft tissue. Reduces nerve compression and relieves muscle spasm.

2

Targeted Exercise Therapy

Specific exercises designed to decompress the sciatic nerve, strengthen the core and glutes, and correct postural imbalances — adapted to your fitness level.

3

Neural Mobilisation

Specialised techniques that gently mobilise the sciatic nerve along its pathway, reducing neural tension and improving nerve function.

4

Dry Needling

Fine needles inserted into trigger points in the piriformis and lumbar muscles to release tension, reduce inflammation, and promote healing.

5

Postural & Ergonomic Education

Many sciatica cases are triggered by poor posture, desk setup, or lifting technique. We provide practical education on safe movement patterns for daily life.

6

Heat & Cold Therapy

Applied during in-clinic sessions to reduce acute inflammation and soothe muscular tension around the nerve pathway.

3 Physiotherapist-Recommended Exercises for Sciatica Relief

Important: Always consult your physiotherapist before starting any exercise program. These exercises are general in nature and may not suit all sciatica presentations.

Exercise 1 — Knee-to-Chest Stretch

  1. Lie flat on your back with knees bent.
  2. Slowly bring one knee up to your chest.
  3. Hold for 20–30 seconds. Repeat on both sides, 3× each.

🎯 Targets: lumbar decompression & piriformis stretch

Exercise 2 — Piriformis Stretch (Figure-4)

  1. Lie on your back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee.
  2. Gently pull the uncrossed leg toward your chest.
  3. Hold 30 seconds. Repeat 3× per side.

🎯 Targets: piriformis & external hip rotators

Exercise 3 — Sciatic Nerve Glide (Seated)

  1. Sit upright in a chair, knees at 90°.
  2. Slowly straighten one leg while pointing toes toward you.
  3. Hold 2 seconds, relax. Repeat 10× per leg.

🎯 Targets: neural mobility & nerve tension reduction

Sciatica Recovery Timeline with Physiotherapy

Recovery varies by individual, but here's a typical progression with consistent physiotherapy:

1

Weeks 1–2

2

Weeks 3–4

Nerve gliding techniques, postural correction, home exercise program established. Pain significantly reduces.

3

Weeks 5–8

Strengthening exercises introduced. Return to daily activities. Nerve symptoms continue to improve.

4

Weeks 8–12

Functional rehabilitation and sport or work-specific exercises. Most patients achieve full recovery.

5

3–6 Months

Maintenance program and prevention strategies. Relapse risk significantly reduced through ongoing conditioning.
